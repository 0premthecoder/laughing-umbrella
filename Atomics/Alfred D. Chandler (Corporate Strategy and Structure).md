# Alfred D. Chandler (Corporate Strategy and Structure)

A business historian who focused on the management and structuring of large, diversified American corporations (such as DuPont, General Motors, and Standard Oil

## Management Theory

- **Structure Follows Strategy:** Chandler's defining thesis. He proved that when a company changes its business strategy, it must inevitably alter its organizational structure. The structure serves as the mechanism for executing the strategy.

- **The multidimensional Form (M-Form) Analysis**: An **M-Form (multidivisional form)** analysis evaluates an organizational structure where a company is divided into semi-autonomous, self-contained divisions based on products, services, or geographic regions

- **The "Visible Hand":** In his Pulitzer Prize-winning book _The Visible Hand_ (1977), Chandler countered Adam Smith’s economic concept of the "invisible hand" (market forces). Chandler argued that it was actually the _visible hand_ of professional management and administrative structure that coordinated large-scale enterprise activities and drove economic progress.

- **Scale and Scope:** Based on an analysis of American, British, and German industrial giants, Chandler concluded that competitive advantage requires simultaneous investment in three areas: production capability (scale), distribution networks (scope), and management capacity.
    
- **Elevating Business History:** Chandler's deep explorations of strategy, structure, and competitive dynamics established business history as a rigorous and respectable academic discipline within business schools.
