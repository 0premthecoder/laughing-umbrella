# Ten Principles of Economics

The 10 principles of economics, as popularized by N. Gregory Mankiw, are:

**People face trade-offs:** To get one thing, you must give up something else.
- [[Society Trade off]]

**The cost of something is what you give up to get it:** This refers to [[opportunity cost]]—the value of the next best alternative foregone.

**[[Rational people]] think at the margin:** Decisions are made by evaluating the incremental benefits and costs of a small change.

**People respond to [[incentives]]:** Behavior changes when the marginal benefits or costs of an action change.

**Trade can make everyone better off:** Specialization and trade allow individuals and nations to consume more than they could by being self-sufficient.

**Markets are usually a good way to organize economic activity:** In a [[market]] economy, the Adam Smith's "invisible hand" of prices and self-interest generally allocates resources efficiently.

**Governments can sometimes improve market outcomes:** Policy interventions can address [[market failures]], such as externalities or the lack of competition.

**A country’s standard of living depends on its ability to produce goods and services:** Productivity—the output produced per unit of labor—is the primary determinant of living standards.

**Prices rise when the government prints too much money:** This principle addresses the cause of inflation or Hyperinflation.

**Society faces a short-run trade-off between inflation and unemployment:** Often referred to as the **Phillips Curve**, this suggests that reducing inflation often causes a temporary increase in unemployment.
